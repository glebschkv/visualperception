   cones and are therefore colour-specific.
3. The consequence of the aperture problem is that:

a) The receptive field of a single neuron never covers the
   whole visual field.

b) The response of a single direction-selective neuron is
   not sufficient to indicate the motion direction of a
   stimulus.

c) Randomly moving stimuli (motion direction coherence
   = 0%) do not elicit a response of a single neuron in the
   middle temporal area.

d) Neurons in the middle temporal area respond
   selectively to a specific motion direction of a stimulus.

a) Perception is error-free.

to the dark adaptation of the eye?

a) Transduction.

b) Rod-cone break.

c) Purkinje effect.

d) Light sensitivity.
7. The psychophysical method of adjustment reveals:

                     Two types of vision

• Photopic vision
      Cone-dominated, foveal and peripheral vision, light-adapted
      vision (insensitive in dim light; operates at high luminance),
      high visual acuity, most sensitive to long wavelengths, basis of
      colour vision


• Scotopic vision
      Rod-dominated, peripheral vision, dark-adapted vision
      (sensitive in dim light; operates at low luminance), low visual
      acuity, sensitive to short wavelengths, no colour sensation

• 3 main types of ganglion cells

   – Parvocellular (P) cells.

• Evidence for two separate streams

   – Ungerleider & Mishkin, 1982

      Monkeys with parietal (‘where’) lesions could distinguish a
      cube from a triangle (object discrimination), while monkeys
      with temporal (‘what’) lesions could not.
      Monkeys with temporal (‘what’) lesions could learn the
      position of an object (location discrimination), while monkeys
      with parietal (‘where’) lesions could not.
      → Perfect double dissociation! Damage of different brain areas
                                        leads to the opposite pattern of
                                        impairments; i.e., those brain areas
                                        have separable functions.

  – Constructive theory of perception

  – Basic idea
     Perception is indirect (interpreted), and a constructive

  – Goal
     Explain how we attach meaning to sensory input.

  – Perception = Interpretation of sensation.

                                  Summary
• We have looked at

   – Real versus illusory motion (e.g., apparent motion, induced motion,
     aftereffects, illusions).
       Only real motion produces retinal motion.

   – Motion-direction selective neurons (V1-V5) that process where an object is
     (evidence from single-cell recordings; Newsome et al., 1995).
       Neuronal response is pooled and there are end-stopped cells to solve the
       aperture problem.
       MT (dorsal) and IT (ventral) are interlinked in the superior temporal sulcus
       (STS), which makes this an expert area for perception and recognition of
       biological motion.

   – The Corollary Discharge Theory which explains motion perception by taking
     retinal motion (image displacement signal) and eye movements (motor
     signal, corollary discharge signal) into account (evidence from single-cell
     recordings; Galletti & Frattori, 2003).

3. The corollary discharge theory
               Corollary Discharge Theory

• Basic idea
  – Our brain knows that we have perceived a moving stimulus,
    because real motion induces retinal motion.
     Successive receptor stimulation on the retina,
     i.e., the object representation wanders across the retina.


• Problem
  – Eye movements also induce retinal motion.
     So, how does our brain know whether real motion has occurred?


• Answer
  – Our brain takes retinal motion as well as eye movements
    into account to determine whether real motion has occurred.
